Greening Ideas

Practical tutorials and actionable steps to bring nature into your space, no matter how small.

Balcony Gardens

Transform your small apartment balcony or condo terrace into a lush, thriving garden. Dealing with tropical heat and monsoon rains requires resilient plants. Growing local favorites like Calamansi, Pechay, or vibrant Bougainvillea is the perfect way to bring the "probinsya" feel to the metro.

Where to Buy Equipment & Plants

  • Local Hubs: Quezon City Memorial Circle plant stalls, Cartimar Market, or Farmers Garden in Cubao.
  • Online: Shopee and Lazada for affordable pots, loam soil, and seeds.
  • Hardware Stores: Ace Hardware or True Value for trellises, watering cans, and gardening gloves.

Examples

A vertical garden using recycled plastic bottles for growing Kangkong and Pechay, or a sun-drenched corner filled with terracotta pots of Sampaguita and Aloe Vera.

Instructions

  • Step 1: Assess your balcony's sunlight (morning sun vs. intense afternoon heat).
  • Step 2: Choose heavy pots that won't blow over during typhoons (Habagat season), ensuring excellent drainage holes.
  • Step 3: Use quality loam soil mixed with coco peat to retain moisture during the dry summer.
  • Step 4: Plant seedlings properly spaced, avoiding overcrowding to prevent pests.
  • Step 5: Water early in the morning or late afternoon to avoid quick evaporation.

Street Greening (Bayanihan Initiative)

Help cool down your barangay and improve air quality by planting trees and shrubs along sidewalks, waiting sheds, and community medians. Let's revive the spirit of "Bayanihan" to create greener streets.

Where to Buy Equipment & Plants

  • DENR Nurseries: The Department of Environment and Natural Resources often provides free seedlings for community projects.
  • Provincial Nurseries: Ideal for buying native trees like Narra, Banaba, or Caballero in bulk.
  • Barangay Hall: Partner with your local LGU to borrow shovels, wheelbarrows, and secure extra soil.

Examples

Planting Yellow Bell or Santan bushes along barangay fences, or a neighborhood initiative to plant shade trees like Mahogany along subdivision main roads.

Instructions

  • Step 1: Secure a permit or coordinate directly with your Barangay Captain and local Homeowners Association (HOA).
  • Step 2: Ensure planting locations do not interfere with Meralco overhead lines or underground water pipes (Maynilad/Manila Water).
  • Step 3: Select native, resilient tree species that can survive both extreme heat and heavy flooding.
  • Step 4: Dig a hole twice the width of the root ball.
  • Step 5: Plant, add organic compost, and assign neighbors a rotation schedule for watering.

Eco-Inspiration

Looking for creative ways to be more sustainable? Explore these upcycling ideas that help reduce waste, save resources, and bring more life into gardens, rooftops, and small urban spaces.

Tire Planters

Use any old car or jeepney tires as an example and stack them to create deep, durable planters for root vegetables, herbs, or large shrubs and trees. Their circular shape helps retain soil, while the thick rubber holds moisture better than many lightweight containers. Tire planters are especially useful for rooftop or compact gardens because they can be arranged in clusters, layered for visual impact, and reused for many growing seasons. They also give discarded tires a second life instead of letting them become waste or breeding sites for pests such as mosquitos and cockroaches .

Plastic Bottle Vertical Wall

Cut unused plastic bottles in half or make an opening and string them along wire mesh or rope to create a space-saving vertical herb wall. This design is ideal for small areas where ground space is limited, yet plants still need sunlight and airflow. Each bottle can hold soil, moss, or any small plants , making it easy to grow basil, mint, parsley, or other compact herbs and any small plants you like . A vertical wall like this also improves visual greenery, reduces plastic waste , and can be adapted to fit fences, balconies, or rooftop edges.

Pallet Raised Beds

Repurpose discarded wooden shipping pallets from markets or any containers that suits you into raised garden beds that improve organization and protect plants from flooding, compacted soil, and ground pests . Raised beds are especially helpful in wet or irregular environments because they allow better drainage and make soil easier to manage . Pallets can be lined with natural fabric or mesh, filled with compost-rich soil, and used for vegetables, flowering plants, or pollinator-friendly species . This method is practical, low-cost, and one of the most efficient ways to turn scrap wood and other items into productive growing space.

Coconut Shell Hanging Pots

Do not throw away buko shells turn them into natural hanging pots by drilling drainage holes and adding twine, wire, or rope for support. Coconut shells are strong, lightweight, biodegradable, and naturally attractive, making them perfect for orchids, ferns, succulents, and trailing plants . They bring a rustic tropical look to gardens while reducing household waste. Because they are small, they work best for low-maintenance plants that do not need large root space or are epiphytes or are air plants , and they are excellent for decorative arrangements in shaded corners, balconies, or hanging displays.

Mini Wetland from Unused Containers

Turn unused containers such as bowls, basins, buckets, and trays into layered shallow wetlands that function as living micro-ecosystems. Instead of being simple water holders, these containers can be arranged with different depths, creating zones for submerged plants, marginal growth, and moist edges. This makes them much more ecologically active because they support aquatic plants, insects, and microorganisms while helping cool the surrounding space. A mini wetland can also serve as a water source for beneficial insects and birds , improve humidity around nearby plants, and bring a small but meaningful wetland habitat into rooftops, patios, or other urban spaces.

Simple Rainwater Catchment Basin

Use a large basin,any container,storage drum , or even a plot of land lined with pond liner or make a pool to collect rainwater from rooftops or a sloped tarpaulin sheet. By directing rainwater flow into one collection point, this system reduces water waste while providing a free and sustainable source of water for plants . The tarpaulin or roof surface acts as a catchment area, guiding water into the basin where it can be stored temporarily or reused for irrigation. This is especially useful in urban environments or seasonal enviroments where rainwater is often lost to drainage systems instead of being reused and there is a dry season where water is critical.
